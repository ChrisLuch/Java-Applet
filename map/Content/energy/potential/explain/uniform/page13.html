Potential Energy in Pendulum Motion

Another system for which energy is conserved is the simple pendulum, as long as air resistance and other friction is negligible.

Run the simulation on the left with the friction equal to zero. (Friction could arise from air resistance and friction at the support point.) The large white rectangle represents the system consisting of the pendulum and the earth. The blue blocks represent the system's potential energy, the red blocks its kinetic energy. Observe how potential energy is converted to kinetic energy during the motion and vice versa so that the total energy KE + PE is always the same.

When do the potential energy and the kinetic energy have their maximum values?

Run the simulation again, this time with friction (you can adjust the friction with the slider) and observe how the sum KE + PE decreases and the dissipated energy DE increases, such that the sum of the three energies, KE + PE + DE, is constant. The dissipated energy is represented by green blocks. They are shown outside the system because we do not consider the air and the pendulum support to be part of the system. (The pendulum bob will initially take up some of the thermal energy generated during the motion, but this will eventually all be conducted away to the surrounding air.)
