Power in Electric Circuits

Answer. Twice as much power is dissipated in the bottom circuit as in the top circuit.

The key to the answer is that, when resistors are connected to a battery in-parallel, the potential difference across each resistor is equal to the battery voltage, 12 V in the present case. The battery voltage is not 'split' in some way between the two resistors. This is a consequence of Kirchhoff's Loop Rule (Voltage Law).

Let V0 denote the battery voltage. Then the power dissipated in each resistor in the bottom circuit is

P = V02/R,

which is also the power dissipated in the one resistor in the top circuit.

Since there are two resistors in the bottom circuit, each dissipating as much power as the resistor in the top circuit, there is twice as much power dissipated in the bottom circuit.

This example illustrates why household wiring is arranged so that electrical appliances in a household are always in-parallel. This way, they all have the same 110-V potential difference across them and dissipate the power they are designed to dissipate. If they were connected in series, the power dissipated by an appliance would depend on what other appliances are connected in-series with it and would be less than what it is designed to dissipate unless it were the only appliance in use in the household.
