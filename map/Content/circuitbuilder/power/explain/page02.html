Power in Electric Circuits

Answer. The power P dissipated in a resistor that has a potential DV across the resistor and a current I in the resistor is given by

P = (DV)I.

For DV = 12 V and I = 2 A, this gives

P = 12x2 = 24 W .

For a derivation of the equation used above, click here.

We could have used another equation, namely,

P = (DV)2/R1 .

The value of R1 is easily found from the definition of resistance to be

R1 = DV/I = 12/2 = 1.2 W.

Thus, the power dissipated in resistor R1 is

P = (DV)2/R1 = 122/1.2 = 24 W.

Question. At what rate is chemical energy converted into electrical energy in the battery and then dissipated in the entire circuit?

Answer. The power of energy conversion in the battery is equal to the battery emf, 100 V in this case, times the current in the battery, 2 A. This amounts to 100x2 = 200 W. By energy conservation, this must equal the power dissipated in the entire circuit.

Where in the circuit is this power dissipated? Wherever there is non-zero resistance in the circuit. Neglecting the resistance in the wires, there is resistance in the two resistors and inside the battery. If the battery is freshly charged, its internal resistance is usually negligible. In that case, the 200 W are dissipated entirely in the two resistors.

One can obtain this result also as follows. If the internal battery resistance is zero, the potential difference across the battery terminals is equal to the battery emf of 100 V. The potential difference between the battery terminals is the potential difference across the two resistors considered as one unit. There is a current of 2 A in this unit. Thus the power dissipated in both resistors together is 100x2 = 200 W.

Question. What power is dissipated in resistor R2?

Since 24 W are dissipated in resistor R1, 200 - 24 = 176 W must be dissipated in resistor R2, because power like energy is an additive quantity. (The power dissipated in resistor R1 added to the power dissipated in resistor R2 is equal to the power dissipated in both resistors together.)

You can verify this result by multiplying the potential difference across resistor R2, which is 100 - 12 = 88 V, by the current in the resistor, 2 A.
