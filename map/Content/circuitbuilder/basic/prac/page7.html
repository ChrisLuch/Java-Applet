DC Circuits

Emf. The emf of a battery is defined as the work done on a charge q when the charge moves through the battery, divided by q.

If there is no 'friction' (internal resistance) inside the battery, causing some of the work done by the battery to be dissipated as heat inside the battery, the battery emf is equal to the potential difference between the battery terminals. If the battery does have internal resistance, the terminal potential difference is less than the emf because the increase in electrical potential energy of the charge when moving through the battery is less than the total work done on the charge by the battery.

The symbol for emf is e. This is the Greek letter 'epsilon'. The SI-unit for emf is the same as for electric potential, namely, the volt (V).

The diagram illustrates a situation that can easily arise when a 12-V car battery is used to start up the engine. The battery terminal potential difference is somewhat less than the battery emf. The emptier the battery gets, the smaller the terminal potential difference will be. This is so because the internal resistance of the battery increases as the battery gets emptier. The emf remains at 12 V.
