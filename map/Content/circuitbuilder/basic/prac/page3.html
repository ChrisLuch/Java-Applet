DC Circuits

Current. The current at some point A in the fluid flow circuit is defined as the rate at which fluid mass passes this point, i.e., as the ratio

(fluid mass past A in time interval Dt)/Dt.

The electric current at some point A in the electric circuit is defined analogously, with 'electric charge' replacing 'fluid mass'.

Note that the current has the same magnitude at all points in these circuits. It is not larger near the top of the pump, nor larger near the bottom of the sloping incline (similarly in the electric circuit). The reason is that both mass and charge are conserved and do not accumulate anywhere. For example, if 0.5 kg are entering at the top of the pipe every second, then 0.5 kg must be leaving the pipe at the bottom every second, and 0.5 kg must be passing point A every second.
