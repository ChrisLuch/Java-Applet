Energy Considerations. In raising the fluid from the bottom to the top, the pump gives it gravitational potential energy. This energy is dissipated in the form of heat when the fluid encounters friction while moving through the sloping narrow pipe. Note that no potential energy is converted to kinetic energy as the fluid moves down the pipe because the constancy of the current along the pipe implies constant fluid speed, assuming the pipe's diameter is constant.

Actually, we will assume that the fluid speeds are small enough that the kinetic energy of the fluid is negligible altogether. A narrow pipe can bring this about. For electric circuits this is an excellent assumption.

What do you think is the speed with which electric charge moves in a typical electric circuit? This may surprise you, but the speed is incredibly small. Its order of magnitude is 1 meter per hour! The speed is not something approaching the speed of light. What is fast is the speed with which changes in one part of a circuit are transmitted to another part. E.g., if a switch is closed in a circuit, the electric charges in a light bulb in the circuit start moving and the bulb starts burning almost immediately, not hours after the switch was closed.
