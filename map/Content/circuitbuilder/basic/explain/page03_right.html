Electric Circuits

Currents. The current at some point A in the fluid flow circuit is defined as the rate at which fluid mass passes this point, i.e., as the ratio

(fluid mass past A in time interval Dt)/Dt.

The electric current at some point A in the electric circuit is defined analogously, with 'electric charge' replacing 'fluid mass'. The SI-unit of electric current is the ampere, or amp for short. Symbol: A. With the coulomb (C) as the SI-unit of electric charge,

1 ampere = (1 coulomb)/(1 second).

Mass and charge conservation. The current has the same value everywhere in these circuits. E.g., in the fluid circuit, the current is the same at the bottom of the incline as at the top. The reason is that mass is conserved. For example, if 0.5 kg of fluid is entering at the top of the sloping pipe every second, then 0.5 kg must be leaving the pipe at the bottom every second, and 0.5 kg must be passing point A every second. Similarly, in the electric circuit, the electric current is the same everywhere because of electric charge conservation.
