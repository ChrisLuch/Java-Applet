Electric Circuits

Conventional Current. Electric currents are not always made of moving negative charges, as in metals. For example, inside a battery, the current consists of positively and negatively charged ions moving in a liquid in opposite directions. By the way, this is one instance where the fluid analogy of electric current fails because the mass of fluid particles can only be positive while electric charge can be both positive and negative.

If positive and negative charges are moving in an electric circuit, they will always move in opposite directions. Does this mean the electric current has two different directions at once?

No! Positive charges moving left have in all respects the same physical effects as negative charges moving right. Using financial transactions as an analogy, giving somebody 100 dollars (positive money) is equivalent to removing a $100 debt (negative money) from that person. The person will have gained equally in both cases.

Therefore one can define a single direction of electric current, the conventional current direction. This is the direction in which equivalent positive charges would move. If a current consists entirely of moving negative charges, the conventional current direction is opposite to the motion of the charges, because positive charges constituting an equivalent current would move oppositely to the negative charges.

It is easiest in most cases to simply forget about whether the charges constituting the current are positive or negative or are a mixture of the two, and to pretend they are all positive.
