Resistors In Series

Answer. The resistance of the two pipes or two light bulbs in series is 2R.

The reason is as follows. First let us consider the fluid flow situation. When the two pipes are in series, the elevation change along each pipe of length a is only half of the total elevation change. As we saw on Page 2, this causes the current to be only half of what it is when a single pipe bridges the entire elevation change. Therefore, the ratio "(potential change)/current" is twice as great when there are two pipes in series. (When the denominator is halved, the ratio is doubled.)

The electrical case is analogous. The electric potential difference is 6 V across the single bulb at the top and the two bulbs in series at the bottom. In the bottom circuit, by Kirchhoff's loop rule (voltage law), the electric potential differences across the two bulbs add. Since they must add to 6 V and since the two bulbs are identical and have the same current, the potential difference across one bulb must be 3 V. The current in a bulb is proportional to the potential difference across it. Thus, the current in each of the bulbs in the bottom circuit must be half that of the current in the bulb in the top circuit. The two ammeter readings confirm this. Again, the ratio "(potential change)/current" is twice as great for the series combination of the two bulbs as for a single bulb.

You may want to try to prove the following more general theorem.

Theorem. The combined resistance R of two resistances R1 and R2 in series is equal to

R = R1 + R2.

A series combination of a 12-W and a 18-W resistor has a resistance of 30 W.
