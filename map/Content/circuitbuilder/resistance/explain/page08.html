Voltage Dividers

Answer. The individual potential differences across two resistors in series must be in the same proportion as the resistances of the two resistors. This is so because both resistors have the same current so that the equation DV = RI, applied to each resistor in turn, implies that DV is proportional to R. Thus, if V1 and V2 denote the potential differences across the top and bottom resistors, we must have

V1/V2 = 4/8 = 1/2 .
We also have
V1 + V2 = 12 .

These are two equations for the two unknowns V1 and V2. They have the solutions

V1 = 4 V and V2 = 8 V ,

which you can verify by substituting these values into the two equations above.

A colloquial expression for electric potential difference is voltage, and a series combination of resistances is called a voltage divider because it divides the total voltage (12 V in the present case) into two parts, in proportion to the resistances combined in series. By choosing appropriate resistances, one can produce any voltage (potential difference) that is a fraction of the given total voltage.
