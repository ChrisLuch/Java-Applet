Electric Potential

Definition

Every static electric field has an electric potential associated with it. Like the electric field, the electric potential is a 'field variable'. This means that the electric potential has a certain value at every point in space and that this value may be different from one point to the next. The electric potential may be constant throughout some region in space.

There is an electric field inside the wires of an electric circuit and inside the other circuit elements. Thus, at every point inside the wires and the other elements of an electric circuit the electric potential has a well-defined value, different values in general at different points in the circuit.

Definition. The electric potential V at a point in space in general, and at a point in an electric circuit in particular, is defined as the ratio

V = PE/q

where PE is the electric potential energy of a test charge q at the given point. The value of the electric potential at a given point does not depend on the value of the test charge at that point because the potential energy is proportional to the test charge so that the value of the test charge cancels.

From the definition above, it is clear that the electric potential at a point is a scalar quantity, because potential energy is a scalar quantity. This is one reason why it is often easier to work with the electric potential than with the electric field. The latter is a vector quantity, which is harder to work with than a scalar quantity.

By dividing the potential energy of a test charge by the test charge, one obtains a quantity that is independent of the test charge and that characterizes the electric conditions that are present at that point in space. (This corresponds to the way the electric field is defined at a point: as the ratio of the electric force acting on a test charge at that point divided by the test change.)

The SI-unit of electric potential is the volt, symbol: V. A volt is equal to (1 J)/(1 C).

Example. The electric potential at a point where a test charge of 2.0x10-6 C has a potential energy of 8.0x10-4 J is equal to

V = (8.0x10-4)/(2.0x10-6) = 400 V .

Potential Difference

The potential difference between two points A and B is simply the difference of the values of the potential at these two points. Thus if the potential at A is 60 V and the potential at B is 40 V, then the potential difference going from A to B is

VB - VA = 40 - 60 = - 20 V.

The potential difference is +20 V if instead of VB - VA one is interested in VA - VB.

Often one wants to know the potential difference between two points in a circuit that are at the opposite ends of a resistor. One then speaks of the 'potential difference across the resistor'.

A colloquial term for potential difference is voltage. Make sure you distinguish between voltage, which is the difference between the potentials at two points, and the potential at a point. There is no such thing as a 'voltage at a point'.

When referring to the 'voltage' between two points one is usually only interested in the magnitude of the potential difference between the two points.

Addition of Potential Differences

Suppose you go from a point A to a point B and then on to a third point C. These may be three points in space or three points in an electric circuit. At each point, the electric potential has a definite value. Let the values at the three points be denoted VA, VB, and VC, respectively. Then it is obvious algebraically that

(VB - VA) + (VC - VB) = VC - VA .

In words, the potential difference between A and B plus the potential difference between B and C is equal to the total potential difference between A and C. Potential differences taken over the segments of a path from A to C add. Thus, if two resistors in a circuit are connected in series, then the total potential difference across both resistors is the sum of the potential differences across the two resistors separately.

Kirchhoff's Loop Rule (Voltage Law)

The sum of the potential differences between a string of points that form a closed loop is equal to zero. (Necessarily, some potential differences will be positive, some will be negative.)

This is a consequence of the fact that the electric potential energy of a test charge has a definite value at a given point in space. Therefore, if one moves a test charge around a closed loop, its potential energy will in general vary along the loop, but return to the value at the starting point when the charge is returned to that point. Thus, there is no overall change in potential energy of the charge as the charge is moved around the loop. The same must then be true for the electric potential, because it differs from the electric potential energy only by a constant factor.

A corollary of Kirchhoff's loop rule is the statement that the sums of potential differences taken along two different paths from a point A to a point B are equal.

Kirchhoff's voltage law is a consequence of energy conservation. If the potential energy of a charge did not return to the original value when the charge returns to the starting point, one could gain energy by moving a charge around a circuit.
