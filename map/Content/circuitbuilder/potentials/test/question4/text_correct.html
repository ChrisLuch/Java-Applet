Correct

Yes, the potential difference across the single resistor on the left is 8 V.

Solution

Plan It

The single resistor on the left is in series with a combination of two resistors in parallel on the right. The potential difference across the in-series arrangement is divided up among the in-series elements in proportion to their resistances.

Set It Up and Solve It

Let each of the three resistors have the resistance R. The combined resistance R|| of the two in-parallel resistors can be determined from the equation

1/R|| = 1/R + 1/R = 2/R

whence
R|| = R/2 .

Thus, the single resistor on the left with a resistance equal to R is in series with a resistance equal to R/2. This implies that the potential difference across the single resistor is twice as large as the potential difference across the in-parallel combination. These two potential differences must add up to 12 V. Thus, it is easy to guess the answer that the potential difference is 8 V across the single resistor and 4 V across the in-parallel combination.

One can also work out this result algebraically. Let us denote the potential differences across the single resistor and the in-parallel combination by Vsingle and V||, respectively. Then the following two equations for these two unknowns are true:

Vsingle + V|| = 12

Vsingle/V|| = Rsingle/R|| = 2.

It is easy to solve these equations for Vsingle. The second equation gives

V|| = 0.5Vsingle.

Substituting this into the first equation gives

Vsingle + 0.5 Vsingle = 1.5 Vsingle = 2

whence
Vsingle = 12/1.5 = 8 V.

Think About It

The current in the single resistor must be equal to the total current in the in-parallel combination, by charge conservation (or Kirchhoff's current law). Each resistor in the in-parallel combination has a voltage of 4 V across it. Therefore, by the definition of resistance, the current in each of these resistors is only half that of the current in the single resistor. This is consistent with the fact that the sum of the currents in the in-parallel resistors must add up to the current in the single resistor.
