Correct

Yes, the potential difference across the 6-W resistor is equal to 4 V.

Plan It. The problem involves two unknown potential differences across the two resistors. We need two equations to find these two unknowns (or even only one of them).

Potential differences along a given path add, and, according to Kirchhoff's loop rule (voltage law), the sum of potential differences around a closed loop is zero. This provides one equation for the potential differences across the two resistors.

Another equation for the two potential differences can be obtained from the fact that the current in two resistors in series is the same, by charge conservation. This current can be related to the potential differences across the two resistors, using the definition of resistance. This yields a second relation between the two potential differences.

Set It Up. From Kirchhoff's loop rule, moving around the circuit in clockwise fashion, we get

12 V - V12 - V6 = 0
or
V12 + V6 = 12 V (1)

where V12 and V6 are the magnitudes of the potential differences across the 12-W and 6-W resistors, respectively.

By the definition of resistance, the common current I in the two resistors can be expressed in terms of the potential differences across and the resistances of these resistors as follows:

I = V12/R12 = V6/R6. (2)

Thus, the two potential differences are in proportion to the two resistances.

Solve It. Substituting numbers into Equation (2) gives

V12/V6 = 12/6 = 2. (3)

It is now easy to solve for the two unknowns, V12 and V6.

Equation (3) gives V12 = 2V6. When this is substituted into Equation (1), V12 is eliminated and we get the following equation for V6:

2V6 + V6 = 3V6 = 12 V
whence
V6 = 4 V. (4)

Think About It. We can check this result as follows. With result (4), Equation (1) gives V12 = 8 V. This is consistent with Equation (3) because the ratio of the two potential differences, 8/4, is indeed equal to 2.

Comment. An arrangement of two or more resistors in series is called a potential divider because the total potential difference across the entire arrangement is divided into parts that add up to the total. The parts are in proportion to the individual resistances. See Equations (1) and (3).
