Electric Potentials and Potential Differences

The voltmeter connected across the bulb reads 22.4 V, and the voltmeter across the open switch 77.6 V. The sum of the two potential differences must be equal to the terminal potential difference of the battery, i.e., 100 V.

In the situation here, the voltmeter readings are determined by the internal resistances of the two meters. The circuit we are dealing with is quite unusual because only the path through the two meters completes the circuit. If one or both meters were removed, there would not be a closed circuit because both the burnt out bulb and the open switch break the circuit.

Since the two meters are in series, they therefore split the total 100 V into two parts according to the ratio of the meters' internal resistances. You can check what these internal resistances are by clicking on the meters with the Select Tool. You can also change these resistances and observe how the voltmeter readings change. (Don't forget to click on the calculator icon after changing a resistance value. This recalculates the circuit.)

Voltmeters have very large internal resistances. This way, if connected in parallel with something like a light bulb that has a much smaller resistance, the meter will draw only an insignificant amount of current and not change the potential difference across the bulb. However, if the bulb is burnt out, the very large resistance of the voltmeter is still smaller than the now almost infinite resistance of the bulb. In this case, the potential difference across the meter depends very much on the resistance of the meter.
