Kirchhoff's Laws

Write down Kirchhoff's Junction Rule (current law) applied at junction B, pretending the currents are in the positive directions indicated in the simulation. (The current in branch 2 is in the negative direction!) This gives the equation

I1 + I2 = I3.

Here, I1 to I3 are the currents in branches 1 to 3, respectively. In accordance with the chosen branch directions, we pretend currents I1 and I2 are entering the junction and I3 is leaving the junction.

Question. How can this equation be correct, knowing that the current in branch 2 is really leaving the junction? Should this current not be on the right-hand side of the equation?

Answer. We can rearrange the equation above to

I1 = (-I2) + I3.

I2 has a negative value because it is downward and therefore in the negative direction. Thus (-I2) is positive. Therefore, all three terms in the preceding equation are positive, and it is clear that this is a correct equation. On the left-hand side we have the only current entering junction B and on the right-hand side we have the two currents leaving the junction, and all three currents are represented by their (positive) magnitudes, I1, (-I2), and I3. Inventing arbitrary positive directions and pretending the currents are in these directions has given a correct equation!

Question. What about the corresponding equation for junction C? Write it down. You will find that it leads to the same equation.

We now have one equation for three currents. In order to be able to calculate the three currents, we need two more equations, so that we have a total of 3 equations for 3 unknowns. The two other equations are provided by Kirchhoff's Loop Rule (voltage law) applied to two different loops in the circuit.
