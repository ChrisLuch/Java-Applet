Yes, IA < IB.

Solution

Plan It. The resistor that has the larger potential difference across it will have the larger current.

Resistors A and B are in series with another resistor or a combination of resistors, respectively. Thus, we have a voltage divider in each circuit, dividing the total potential difference across the respective arrangements of resistors. In both cases, the total potential difference is equal to the battery emf, V0. The voltage V0 is divided in proportion to the resistances in series. (See Electric Circuits/Resistance/Explain It.)

Set It Up and Solve It. Let the common resistance of all resistors be R. In the top circuit, resistor A has another resistor of the same resistance R in series with it. Thus the potential difference across each resistor is V0/2.

The resistance of the combination of two in-parallel resistors in the bottom circuit is less than R. In fact, it is equal to R/2. (See Resistance under Get Information. You will find an equation that lets you calculate this resistance. You can also review the theory of in-parallel resistors in Electric Circuits/Resistance/Explain It.)

The potential differences across resistors in series are proportional to the respective resistances. Thus, in the bottom circuit, the potential difference across resistor B is twice that across the in-parallel combination of resistors, or (2/3)V0 across resistor B vs. (1/3)V0 across the in-parallel combination. (2/3)V0 is more than the (1/2)V0 across resistor A. Thus, the current in resistor B is larger than that in resistor A.

Think About It. Each of the two in-parallel resistors in the bottom circuit has a potential difference of V0/3 across it. This is half the 2/3V0 potential difference across resistor B. Thus, the current in each of the in-parallel resistors is half that in resistor B, and together the currents in the in-parallel resistors add up to that in resistor B. This is as it should be according to the Kirchhoff junction rule.
