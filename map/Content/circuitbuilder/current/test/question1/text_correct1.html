Correct

Yes, bulb B burns brighter than bulb A.

Solution

Plan It. The bulb that has the larger potential difference across it burns brighter. Bulbs A and B have the same resistance. Given the resistance, the bulb with the greater potential difference DV across it has more current I, and dissipates more power: P = (DV) I.

Bulb A in the top circuit is in series with another bulb, and bulb B in the bottom circuit is in series with a combination of two bulbs. Thus, in each circuit we have a voltage divider dividing the total voltage (potential difference) across the entire combination of light bulbs in the circuit.

In the top circuit, the total voltage is divided into a part across bulb A and a part across the other bulb in that circuit. In the bottom circuit, the total voltage is divided into a part across bulb B and a part across the parallel combination of the other two light bulbs in that circuit.

Both circuits have the same total total voltage across their respective combinations of bulbs. This total voltage is equal to the battery emf, V0, assuming the battery has no internal resistance. The voltage V0 is divided in proportion to the resistances of the in-series circuit elements. (See Electric Circuits/Resistance/Explain It.)
