Electric Current

On the left are shown three identical circuits. All bulbs are identical. There are three switches in each circuit, and the only thing distinguishing the circuits is whether the switches are open or closed.

Question. Can you explain the brightness of the three bulbs and the currents in each of the three circuits?

Top circuit - all three switches are closed. The two bulbs in the right branch are dimmer than the single bulb in the left branch. Reason: The potential difference across the single bulb in the left branch is 6 V. The total potential difference across the two bulbs in the right branch is also 6 V. However, in the right branch, the 6-V potential difference is split up into 3 V across the first bulb and 3 V across the second bulb. (See Page 9.) With only 3 V potential difference across each bulb, the bulbs in the right branch will have less current (0.3 A) than the bulb in the left branch (0.6 A) and burn dimmer.

Middle circuit - the switch in the right branch is open. There is no current in the right branch, because of the open switch, so the bulbs in this branch don't burn. The bulb in the left branch is as bright as in the top circuit because it has the same potential difference of 6 V across it and therefore the same current (0.6 A) as in the top circuit.

Bottom circuit - the switch in the left branch is open. Now there is no current in the left branch and the bulb in that branch does not burn. The bulbs in the right branch burn as in the top circuit because they have the same total 6-V potential difference across them as in the top circuit, split into 3-V potential differences per bulb. Since the potential differences across each bulb are the same as in the top circuit, the currents in each bulb (0.3 A) are the same as well in both circuits.
