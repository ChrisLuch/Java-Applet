Normal Force

Observe that the normal force is not equal to the weight of the object. For the 2.0-kg object on the left, the normal force is 23.6 N while the weight is 19.6 N, and for a 60-kg object the normal force is 708 N while the weight is equal to 60x9.8 = 588 N, always assuming a 2.0 m s-2 upward acceleration.

An important lesson for you to learn is that in general the normal force is not equal in magnitude to the object's weight, because the normal force is not the reaction force to the gravitational force, in the sense of Newton's third law. If these two forces were an action-reaction pair, their magnitudes would have to be equal no matter how the object is accelerating.

The normal force varies with the object's acceleration and must be determined using Newton's second law, as in the calculation on Page 3. In contrast, the object's weight always is 19.6 N no matter how the object is accelerating. The same is true for the reaction force to the force of gravity acting on the object, namely, the upward force that the object exerts on the entire earth. This reaction force always has the same magnitude of 19.6 N equal to the object's weight, no matter what the object's acceleration is.
