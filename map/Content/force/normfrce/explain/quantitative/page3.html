Normal Force

Explanation. Let the explanation be in terms of the situation simulated on the left, and let us do the calculation in the Lab frame, which is an inertial frame so that Newton's second law holds in this frame.

The free-body diagram for the 2.0-kg object is shown on the far left. Two forces are acting on the object, the downward gravitational force of magnitude W and the upward normal force of magnitude N. The gravitational force is exerted on the object by the earth, and the normal force is exerted on the object by the springs. The normal force is a contact force. Since nothing besides the springs is in contact with the 2.0-kg object, there are no other contact forces acting on the object. (It is true that the air is in contact with the object, but let us ignore the forces exerted on the object by the air.)

We can calculate the normal force by applying Newton's second law to the 2.0-kg object. Considering the y-components gives

N - W = ma

where a is the y-component of the acceleration. Substituting W = mg and solving for N gives

N = m(g + a) .

Thus, for a = 2.0 m s-2 and m = 2.0 kg we get

N = 2.0 x (9.8 + 2.0) = 23.6 N .

Analogously, for m = 60 kg one gets

N = 60 x (9.8 + 2.0) = 708 N

which amounts to an equivalent mass of

mequ = 708/9.8 = 72.2 kg .
