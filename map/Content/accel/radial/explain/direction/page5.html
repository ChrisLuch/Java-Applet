Direction of the Acceleration in Uniform Circular Motion

Final Remarks

  • The image shows two copies of the acceleration vector of a particle performing uniform circular motion. One copy is attached to the tip of the velocity vector, as in the image on Pages 1 and 3. The other copy is attached to the particle. It is the latter copy that shows most clearly why the direction of the acceleration is described as "radial" or "centripetal". The word "centripetal" is derived from Latin and means "center-seeking".

    Note, however, that if the acceleration vector is attached to the center of the circle like the velocity vector, its direction is away from the center rather than towards it. Its direction is still radial. The applet on Page 4 can display the acceleration vector when it is attached to the center of the circle. Select "acceleration at origin" from the Vector Panel.

    An unambiguous way of stating what the direction of the acceleration vector is to say that its direction is opposite to that of the position vector.

  • The reasoning presented here that shows that the acceleration vector points in the direction of motion of the tip of the velocity vector, when that vector's tail end is fixed, and therefore in the direction of the tangent to the velocity path applies also when the motion is not uniform. This case is considered under Kinematics/Acceleration/Tangential/. You can explore it on your own with the applet on Page 4 by setting dv/dt to a non-zero value.
