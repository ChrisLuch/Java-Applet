Torque and Rotation

In general, when one or several forces are acting on a rigid object, the resulting motion is a combination of

TRANSLATION + ROTATION

where by TRANSLATION is meant the motion of the CM and by ROTATION a rotation around the CM. The CM plays a very special role in describing the general motion of a rigid object. (If an object is not rigid, additional vibrational motions of the object are possible that change the object's shape. Think of a drop of fluid, for example.)

Let us study the effect of just a single force, as simulated here.

In the present setup, where the single force applied to the object is downward, the CM carries out a parabolic translational motion that is just like that of an object in the earth's downward gravitational field (so-called projectile motion). Quite generally, parabolic motion of the CM is the result of a constant net force acting on an object, and an initial velocity that is not in the direction of the net force.

The object shown here oscillates as its CM moves on a parabolic path. An object moving in the earth's gravitational field does not oscillate. Why not?

Reset the applet and drag the force vector so that it acts at a different point, but change neither the magnitude nor the direction of the force. Then PLAY the simulation again. The CM will move along the same parabolic path no matter where the force is acting on the object!

However, the oscillatory (rotational) aspect of the motion does depend on where the force is acting. Drag the force vector so that it acts at the CM, and observe the resulting motion.

You should again observe parabolic translational motion, but this time without any rotation, just as in projectile motion in the earth's gravitational field. Why are there no oscillations this time? Note that the action of the earth's gravitational force on an object is such that the force can be thought of as acting at the object's CM. Still, why does a force that is acting at the CM not produce any rotation?
