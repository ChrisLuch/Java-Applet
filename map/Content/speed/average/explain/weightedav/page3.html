Time-weighted Average of Speeds

Calculation of average speed in general

Let us define some notation first.

s1 = first distance traveled (half-way around the oval)

t1 = corresponding time elapsed

v1 = speed for first half of oval

The corresponding quantities for the second half of the oval are denoted s2, t2, and v2, and let t denote the total time elapsed for going around the entire oval:

t = t1 + t2.

We will now derive a general expression for the average speed of two speeds in terms of these symbols. Values that apply to a specific situation, like that on Page 2, can then be 'plugged' into this expression.

vav = (distance around the oval) / (total time elapsed)

= (s1+s2) / t. (1)

From the definition of average speed it also follows that

s1 = t1×v1 ands2 = t2×v2.(2)

Substituting Eqs.(2) into Eq.(1) gives

vav = (t1v1 + t2v2) / t

= (t1 / t) v1 + (t2 / t) v2.(3)

Important observation. In Expression (3) for the average speed, the factors multiplying the individual speeds are not equal to ½, but equal to the fractions of the total time elapsed while the speeds v1 and v2 are maintained, namely, t1/t and t2/t, respectively.

E.g., if during two thirds of the total time elapsed the speed is equal to v1 and during one third of the total time elapsed the speed is equal to v2, the average speed is equal to

vav = 2/3 v1 + 1/3 v2.(4)

This is not equal to the arithmetic mean of the two speeds, which is (v1 + v2) / 2 = ½ v1 + ½ v2.

Expression (3) for the average speed was derived without any special assumptions. Therefore it applies in any situation in which the average of two speeds needs to be calculated.

Expression (3) is called a time-weighted average. Thus, average speed is a time-weighted average, not an arithmetic mean of speeds. This means that the speed that is maintained the longest makes the largest contribution to the average speed.

Evaluation of the average speed for the situation examined on Page 2

The speeds during the first and second halves of the oval, respectively, were

v1 = 12.0 m/sandv2 = 24.0 m/s. (5)

Since the distances traveled in both halves are equal, the times elapsed are inversely proportional to the speeds. Since v1 = ½ v2, we therefore have t1 = 2 t2, i.e., the car takes twice as much time to complete the first half of the oval as for the second half. Therefore,

t1 / t = 2/3 and t2 / t = 1/3.(6)

These are just the factors in Eq.(4). Thus, Eq.(4) applies in the present case. Substituting the given values for the speeds gives

vav = 2/3 v1 + 1/3 v2 = 2/3×12.0 + 1/3×24.0 = 16.0 m/s.(7)

In contrast, the arithmetic mean of the two speeds, which is not equal to the average speed, is equal to (12 + 24) / 2 = 18 m/s. Since the smaller one of the two speeds, 12 m/s, is maintained longer than the larger one, 24 m/s, the average speed is closer to 12 m/s than to 24 m/s. In contrast, the arithmetic mean is exactly half-way between the two speeds.
