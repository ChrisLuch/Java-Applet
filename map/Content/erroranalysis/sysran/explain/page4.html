Handling Systematic Error

Some common examples of systematic error found in physics experiments and calculations might be:

  • using a metre stick that's calibrated incorrectly
  • forgetting to find (and use) the zero reading on a micrometer
  • merely cutting off insignificant digits instead of rounding them properly

So now the question becomes: How does one minimize these errors? The short answer is, be careful. Most systematic error comes from improper use of equipment or from careless calculations. Make sure you read scales properly, perform calculations carefully, and, when in doubt, talk to somebody.

Keep in mind, though, that with all the care in the world, you'll probably never truly get rid of systematic error and that you will always have to remain on the lookout for this kind of error.

If you keep looking for systematic error in your experiments, you may become aware of sources of systematic error that you can try to eliminate or, if that is not possible, try to quantify so that you can make appropriate corrections in your calculations.
