A Summary

There are a few things to keep in mind when deciding how many significant figures to keep:

  • Keep all the digits your calculator gives you, and round at the very end.
    If you round to, say, one significant digit every time, you're going to lose a lot of information. If you keep all these digits, your final result will most likely be more accurate.
  • Use the right uncertainty formula.
    Double-check the formula before you barrel ahead and use it. If it so satisfies you, derive it from first principles.
  • Keep absolute and relative uncertainties straight.
    A good way to do this is by carefully keeping track of units -- if you divide something in centimetres by something else in centimetres, the units will cancel and the number is dimensionless (i.e., a ratio).
  • Uncertainties contain either one or two nonzero digits.
    There's no point in saying "the uncertainty in the time is 1.5532432 s -- the extra digits are superfluous. Same goes for relative (or percentage) uncertainties -- 0.77986% is a little excessive.
  • A quantity's number of significant digits depends on its uncertainty.
    If your uncertainty is 0.5 m, don't bother to say the measurement is 22.0477 m -- everything past the tenths is uncertain anyways.
