Propagation of Error
Addition and Subtraction

So far, we have looked at examples of error propagation for functions of a single variable. Now, let us look at examples involving functions of two variables. The same Max/Min Method can be used, but now both variables must be varied to determine the maximum and minimum values of the function.

The most important examples we need to consider are the four elementary operations of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division of two variables. Addition and subtraction will be dealt with on the present Page 5, multiplication and division on the following Page 6.

Addition of Two Quantities

Question. A realtor measures the length of a house with a measuring tape. The tape is not long enough to measure the entire length and she has to use the tape twice, obtaining the two partial lengths L1 = 8.345 m and L2 = 6.328 m. The uncertainty in each measurement is 0.003 m. What is the length L of the house and its uncertainty?

Answer. The lengths L1 and L2 will be in the ranges

(8.345 - 0.003) m < L1 < (8.345 + 0.003) m

(6.328 - 0.003) m < L2 < (6.328 + 0.003) m.

Adding the least values will give the least value for L and adding the largest values will give the largest value for L. Thus we obtain for L the range

(14.673 - 0.006) m < L < (14.673 + 0.006) m .

Another way of stating this result is by writing

L ± DL = (14.673 ± 0.006) m.

Clearly, the absolute uncertainties in the two partial lengths add,

DL = DL1 + DL2

Subtraction of Two Quantities

Question. A moving company weighs the mass m1 -- the company will probably not call this mass but will call it weight -- of its empty moving van to be 6,352 kg and the mass m2 of the loaded van to be 9,427 kg. Both measurements have an absolute uncertainty of 4 kg. What is the mass m of the contents?

Anwer. The mass m is the difference of the two masses,

m = m2 - m1.

To obtain the largest possible value for the mass of the contents, we take the largest possible value of the loaded van and subtract from it the smallest possible value of the empty van,

m2 - m1 < (9,427 + 4) kg - (6,352 - 4) kg = (3,075 + 8) kg .

Similarly, to obtain the smallest possible value for the mass of the contents, we take the smallest possible value of the loaded van and subtract from it the largest possible value of the empty van,

(9,427 - 4) kg - (6,352 + 4) kg = (3,075 - 8) kg < m2 - m1.

Thus the difference of the two masses and therefore the mass of the contents is in the range

(3,075 - 8) kg < m2 - m1 < (3,075 + 8) kg .

The same result can also be stated in the form

m ± Dm = (3,075 ± 8) kg .

Comment. When subtracting two quantities, the absolute uncertainties of the two quantites add to give the uncertainty of the difference. They do not subtract. In the present case, if the uncertainties subtracted, the uncertainty of the mass m would be equal to 4 - 4 = 0 kg, which is clearly incorrect.
