Absolute and Relative Error
A Preview

As explained in Errors/Basic Facts/Explain It, we always must include a certain amount of error or uncertainty when reporting a measurement result. This is a fact of life -- we can't know physical quantities, e.g., lengths and times, exactly. Our equipment and our senses have inherent limitations in them.


The fact that the scientist isn't even looking at the test tube must be a source of error.

There are two main ways to represent uncertainties: in an absolute sense, and in a relative sense. The first method is the most straightforward... if someone says, "My height is 68 inches, give-or-take one inch," that "give-or-take" value of one inch is your absolute uncertainty. However, if someone says, "My mass is 70 kg, give-or-take 5%," the "5%" is a relative uncertainty, since the actual (absolute) uncertainty (in kilograms) depends on the person's mass -- the heavier the person, the greater the absolute uncertainty if the relative uncertainty is always 5%.

Absolute error can be converted into relative error and vice versa. So both contain the same amount of information. However, sometimes it is more convenient to present the information in one way rather than the other. E.g., if you want to convey an impression of the accuracy of a result, an uncertainty of 0.1% suggests a pretty accurate result. However, if you report the corresponding absolute uncertainty as 2 m, then this does not look very accurate at all, until you point out that the measured quantity has a length of 2000 m.
