Power in Mechanical Systems

Work is a transfer of mechanical energy. Therefore, the mechanical input to our output from a system is equal to the rate in time at which work is being done on or by the system.

One-dimensional motion

Suppose the system consists of a particle moving along an x-axis and that it has a force acting on it that is pointing in the positive or negative x-direction with x-component Fx. At a given instant, the particle has an x-component of velocity vx so that the x-component dx of the particle's displacement during the infinitesimal time interval dt is equal to vxdt.

The work dW done on the particle during the infinitesimal time interval is

dW = Fxdx.

Therefore the power transfer to the particle by the given force, i.e., the rate at which the force is doing work on the particle (transmitting energy to the particle) is equal to

P = dW/dt = Fx dx/dt = Fx vx .

The power is positive, i.e., energy is being added to the particle, if the force and velocity are in the same direction. The power is negative, i.e., energy is being taken away from the particle, if the force and velocity are in opposite directions.

Example. If at a given instant a force Fx = 20 N is acting on a particle moving with velocity vx = -5.0 m/s, the power transferred to the particle at that instant is P = 20x(-5.0) = - 100 W. The negative value means that energy is being transmitted at a negative rate, i.e., that the particle's energy is decreasing.

If several forces are acting on the particle at once, the total power is the sum of the powers due to the individual forces.
