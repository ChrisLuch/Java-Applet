Power in Electrical Circuits

DC Circuits

Suppose we have a resistor with resistance R and current I. The potential difference across the resistor is DV = RI. Then the power dissipated in the resistor is given by any of the following three expressions:

P = (DV)I = (DV)2/R = RI2 .

If there is a current I in a battery of emf V0, the electrical power delivered by the battery to the circuit is equal to

P = V0I .

AC Circuits

The preceding expressions apply also to AC circuits if all quantities that are time-variable in the AC case, namely, potential differences, currents, and emf's, are replaced by their rms-values. An rms-value is a certain kind of average ('rms' stands for root-mean-square, and 'mean' is another word for 'average'.)

E.g., the 110-V potential difference between the poles of a household plug is the rms-value of the time-variable potential difference between the two poles. The potential difference between the poles goes from 155 V to -155 V back to 155 V, fifty times every second. The rms-value of the potential difference between the poles is equal to 155 V divided by the square root of 2.
