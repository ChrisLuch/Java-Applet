Acceleration

Definition

Acceleration is the time-rate-of-change of velocity.

What does this mean?

In general, the acceleration vector points in a different direction than the velocity vector, because the change of the velocity vector is in general not in the same direction as the velocity vector itself. See the simulations on Pages 2 and 5.

Acceleration is a vector quantity because velocity is a vector, and therefore a change in velocity divided by a scalar (the time elapsed) a vector also. The acceleration vector points in the direction in which the velocity vector is changing. This is the direction in which the tip of the velocity vector is moving if the tail end is fixed.

The relationship between acceleration and velocity is exactly like that between velocity and position. Remember that velocity is the time-rate-of-change of the position vector. The simulations on Pages 5 to 7 will be based on this analogy.

An object's acceleration is non-zero whenever the object's velocity is changing. Note that acceleration is not defined as the time-rate-of-change of speed, which would make it a scalar quantity. What difference does that make?

With acceleration defined as the time-rate-of-change of velocity, the acceleration of a particle is non-zero whenever the particle is moving on a curved trajectory even though the particle's speed may be constant. The vector nature of acceleration is crucial in many contexts, e.g., when trying to explain the motion of a planet in terms of Newton's laws.
