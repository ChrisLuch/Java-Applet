Acceleration

The applet lets you control the motion of a car by controlling the car's acceleration by means of an acceleration dial. For detailed information on how to use the applet, please go to Applet Help on the applet's Help menu.

Currently the Velocity Space check box is checked. This means the applet will show the motion in velocity space, which is the space of velocity vectors. The dotted background suggests position space. When you start the motion, the car will stay at a fixed position at the origin of velocity space, but you will see the position space background moving relative to the car.

Set the car's initial velocity by clicking at the car's center and dragging the tip of the magenta velocity vector.

The orange dot at the tip of the velocity vector represents the car's acceleration vector, which is zero initially. As soon as you click somewhere in the Acceleration Dial, this vector will become non-zero and the motion will start. It will continue as long as you keep the mouse button depressed.

Vary the acceleration by dragging the acceleration vector in the acceleration dial, and observe how the car's velocity changes.

To see the relationship between the orange acceleration vector and the magenta velocity vector more clearly, uncheck the Car checkbox.

Can you observe that the relationship between the acceleration and velocity vectors is exactly like that between velocity and position vectors?

For a review of the latter, go to Velocity/Basic Facts/Simulate It. Imagine that the labels 'Velocity Space' and 'Acceleration Dial' are changed to 'Position Space' and 'Velocity Dial', respectively, and that the velocity vector is labeled position vector and the acceleration vector is labeled velocity vector. With these name changes, the present simulation performs exactly like the simulation in Velocity/Basic Facts/Simulate It.

Lesson To Be Learned

As the time-rate-of-change of the position vector is equal to the velocity vector, so the time-rate-of-change of the velocity vector is equal to the acceleration vector.
