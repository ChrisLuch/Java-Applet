Power in Electrical Circuits

Power dissipated in DC circuits

Suppose we have a resistor with resistance R and current I. The potential difference across the resistor is DV = RI. During an infinitesimal time interval dt, an infinitesimal amount of charge dq is entering the resistor at one end and an equal amount of charge is leaving the resistor at the other end. Then the electrical potential energy transformed into thermal energy in the resistor during this time interval is

|dPE| = |DV| dq .

Thus, the power P dissipated in the resistor is

P = |dPE|/dt = |DV| dq/dt = |DV| I .

Example. A light bulb that has a 120-V potential difference across it has a current of 0.625 A. The power dissipated in the bulb is P = 120x0.625 = 75 W.

Useful Equations. Combining the equation for power dissipation in a resistor with the definition of resistance, one can calculate this power from any of the following three expressions:

P = (DV)I = (DV)2/R = RI2 .

AC Circuits

The preceding expressions apply also to AC circuits if all quantities that are time-variable in the AC case, namely, potential differences, currents, and emf's, are replaced by their rms-values. An rms-value is a certain kind of average ('rms' stands for root-mean-square, where 'mean' is another word for 'average'.)

E.g., the 120-V potential difference between the poles of a household plug is the rms-value of the time-variable potential difference between the two poles. Fifty times every second, the potential difference between the poles goes from 170 V to -170 V back to 170 V. The rms-value of the potential difference between the poles is equal to 170 V divided by the square root of 2.

All values for potential differences, currents, and powers in AC circuits are usually quoted as rms values, unless something to the contrary is explicitly stated. Thus, a 120-V household bulb rated at 60 W has a current I = 60/120 = 0.5 A, where all values are rms-values.
