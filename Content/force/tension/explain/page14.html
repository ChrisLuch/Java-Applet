Derivation of T/F = m/M

In the equation in the title of this page, T is the tension in one of the strings and m is the combined mass of all blocks 'behind' that string. M = m1+m2+m3 is the total mass of all three blocks, and F is the forward force applied to the system.

Let us assume there is friction between the blocks and the surface they slide on. To make things simple, the simulation is designed so that all blocks have the same coefficient of friction, m, and that m has the same value in the static and kinetic cases. Also, the acceleration due to gravity, g, is taken to have the value 10.0 m s-2.

When the system is in motion, the total friction force acting on the system has a magnitude of mMg. Since the friction force is opposite to the applied force F, Newton's second law applied to the entire system gives

F - mMg = Ma
whence
F = M [a + mg] .

Exactly the same kind of reasoning leads to an analogous expression for the tension in, say, the middle string,

T2 = (m2+m3) [a + mg] .

One just has to replace F by T2 and M by m2+m3. The idea is that the subsystem consisting of the last two blocks has a forward force acting on it equal to the tension T2, just as the entire system of three blocks is pulled by the force F, and a retarding force equal to the combined friction force acting on the last two blocks, m(m2+m3)g.

Dividing the left-hand and right-hand sides of the equations for T2 and F gives the desired result,

T2/F = (m2+m3)/M.

It may surprise you that according to this the tension is independent of the coefficient of friction. Does that makes sense?

Here is a reason. The tension increases from 0 to F from the rear end to the front end of the chain in proportion to the amount of mass that lies behind the point at which the tension is evaluated. There is no room for friction in this proportion. E.g., it cannot possibly be true that, because of friction, the tension is greater everywhere by a certain factor, because then the tension at the front end of the chain would be greater than F, which is impossible.
