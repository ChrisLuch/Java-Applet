Normal Force

Answer. The so-called 'weight' shown by the bathroom scale is really the normal force exerted by the scale on whatever object is resting on the scale.

If the object is a 2.0-kg object and the scale and object have an upward 2.0-m s-2 acceleration, this normal force has a magnitude of 23.6 N. (If the object's mass is 60 kg, the magnitude of the normal force under the same conditions is 708 N.)

Although ordinary bathroom scales measure forces, they are not calibrated in newton, but in kilogram (or sometimes in lb, but let's assume kg here). They show a kind of equivalent mass, mequ, equal to the normal force divided by the magnitude of the acceleration due to gravity, g = 9.8 m s-2. For a 60-kg object with a 2.0-m s-2 upward acceleration,

mequ = N/g = 708/9.8 = 72.2 kg.

Of course, the true mass of an object does not depend on the acceleration, but the equivalent mass does because it is a really a normal force, calibrated in terms of mass. For an explanation of why it is the normal force that is measured by a bathroom scale and how to calculate the normal force, go on to the next few pages.
