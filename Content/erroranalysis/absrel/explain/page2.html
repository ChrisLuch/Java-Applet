Absolute Error

These types of errors -- with the same units as the measured quantity, as in the case of the applet on the left -- are called absolute errors (or "absolute uncertainties"). If the length of the object is denoted L, then the absolute error in L is denoted DL. By definition, an absolute error is always positive. Compare the comments under "Notation" on Page 1 of Errors/Basic Facts/Explain It.

Reading scales.

Choosing an amount of error in a situation like that on the left may seem a bit arbitrary to you. However, there are some guidelines you can follow for reading scales.

Given ideal conditions -- the ruler is in good shape, the room is bright, the markings are clearly visible, etc. -- a scale can be read down to one-tenth of its smallest division (e.g., ± 0.1 cm on a centimetre ruler). However, this is more of a judgement call than anything. If you think you can only read the scale to within the nearest fifth or half of a division, then go with that as your uncertainty.
