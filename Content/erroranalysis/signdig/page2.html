Calculations with Sig. Digits

Now we'll calculate the Object's area, and express it in the form A ± sA, where sA is the absolute uncertainty in A.

The absolute uncertainty in each of the measurements was 0.02 cm -- that fact alone should tell us that there should be some amount of uncertainty in the Object's area.

As we've seen before, the relative uncertainties of quantities are squared, summed, and square-rooted when these same quantities are multiplied. To find the relative uncertainties, divide the absolute uncertainties by the quantity:

sL / L = (0.02 cm) / (1.18 cm) = 0.016949...
sW / W = (0.02 cm) / (0.19 cm) = 0.10526...
(The "..."'s are left to show that you should keep all the digits your calculator gives for now, and round them to a certain number of digits at the very end. This minimizes rounding errors.)

Now we can calculate the area, neglecting the uncertainty for now:

A = LW = (1.18 cm)(0.19 cm) = 0.2242 cm2

If we square each of the relative uncertainties and add them, we get 0.011367..., and square-rooting gives us 0.10661... (notice how this uncertainty is "dominated" by the proportionately large relative uncertainty in W). This, of course, is the relative uncertainty in A.

Now we want to express the area as A ± sA, which means we have to find the absolute uncertainty in A. We get this by multiplying sA / A by our value for A:

sA = (0.10661...)(0.2242 cm2) = 0.02390... cm2...

...making our final value for A = 0.2242 ± 0.02390... cm2. I think you'll agree those extra digits hanging off the end of our statement look rather unsightly, so our next task is to find out how many digits we should keep and which ones to toss away.
