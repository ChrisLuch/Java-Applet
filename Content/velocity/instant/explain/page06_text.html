Instantaneous Velocity

Velocity Vs. Speed

At any given instant, the speed of an object is equal to the magnitude of the object's velocity.

The speed of an object is defined as the time-rate-of-change of the distance traveled by the object. It turns out that this is equal to the magnitude of the velocity. Can you explain why this is true?

The answer is not obvious, because the average speed over some finite time interval is in general not equal to the magnitude of the average velocity. See Velocity/Average/Explain It for a simulation and discussion of that fact. Then why is it that when dealing with instantaneous quantities the speed is equal to the magnitude of the velocity?

Answer

The simulation on this page will illustrate the idea underlying the answer. Click the PLAY or STEP buttons of the applet. The car will move in a circle. The simulation will show the car's displacement vectors during successive 0.04-s time intervals in green.

Each one of these displacement vectors is a chord to the circular path, connecting the end points of the corresponding arc element of the path. Since each displacement is small, each is indistinguishable graphically from the corresponding arc and its magnitude very close to the length of that arc. Dividing the magnitude of one of these displacements or the length of the corresponding arc by the time interval dt = 0.04 s gives the magnitude of the average velocity or the average speed, respectively, during this time interval. Thus, the latter quantities are almost equal as well. In the limit as the time interval goes to zero they become exactly equal.
