Ohm's Law

In the simulation on the left, the current in a resistor and the potential difference across it are proportional to each other. You can check this by varying the battery emf and observing DV and I for the resistor.

This means that the resistance of the resistor, R = (DV)/I, is constant. The statement that R is constant as the current is varied is known as Ohm's Law.

Metals obey Ohm's Law, as long as the temperature is constant. As the temperature increases, the resistance of a metal increases. Below a certain temperature, some metals have no resistance at all and are then said to be superconducting. Thus, metals do not obey Ohm's law if one allows their temperature to vary.

The variation of temperature and resistance is very noticeable in light bulbs. The resistance of a light bulb increases quite a bit as the current increases from when the filament is glowing red to when it is white hot.

Our electric circuit simulation assumes that all circuit elements, wires, resistors, batteries, and light bulbs obey Ohm's Law. For light bulbs this is quite unrealistic, but this technicality is not important for learning the concepts that the simulation is designed to teach. It would only complicate matters, unnecessarily, to treat light bulbs more realistically. (It would also make the simulation quite a bit harder to program.)

Note that one defines the resistance of a circuit element as R = (DV)/I, whether R is constant or not, and that this equation must be distinguished from Ohm's Law.

Ohm's Law is often stated in the form

DV = RI.

However, this equation is really no more than the definition of resistance. Therefore, the equation is correct whether R is constant or not. However, if R is constant or, equivalently, if DV and I are proportional to each other, then the circuit element obeys Ohm's Law.
