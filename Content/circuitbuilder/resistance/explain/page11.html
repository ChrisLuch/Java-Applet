Resistance

The two circuits, the top one with a single bulb and the bottom one with two bulbs in parallel (both identical to the top bulb), are identical to the circuits shown on Pages 9 and 10, except that in the bottom circuit ammeters have been inserted into the parallel branches. The readings of these meters confirm that the current in each bulb in the parallel set-up is identical to that of the single bulb in the top circuit. Adding a second bulb in parallel to the first one does not change the conditions affecting the first bulb.

Question. What happens to the potential difference across and the current in the first bulb in the bottom circuit if the second bulb is not identical to but different from it? Try it by changing the design voltage of the second bulb from 6 V to 3 V (by selecting the Select Tool and then clicking on the second bulb and changing the design voltage in the display below).

Answer. The conditions affecting the first bulb do not change. If you have changed the second bulb correctly, the current in it is increased from 0.6 A to 2.4 A and the total current in the circuit from 1.2 A to 3.0 A, but the potential difference across and the current in the first bulb are unchanged, 6 V and 0.6 A, respectively.

Question. What is the resistance of the parallel combination of the two bulbs now? The first bulb has a resistance of 10 W and the second one now has a resistance of 2.5 W. (See the display of values when the bulb is selected.)

Answer. The resistance R of the parallel combination of the two bulbs is

R = (DV)/I = (6 V)/(3 A) = 2 W.

In general, if two resistors are connected in parallel whose resistances are R1 and R2, the resistance R of the parallel combination is given by the equation

1/R = 1/R1 + 1/R2 .

You may want to try to prove this general theorem. Let's verify it for the case considered here. For R1 = 10 W and R2 = 2.5 W it gives

1/R = 1/10 + 1/2.5 = O.5 W-1.

Thus, R = 2 W, as above.
