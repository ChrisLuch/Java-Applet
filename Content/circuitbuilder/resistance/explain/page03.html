The Concept of Resistance

Now let us turn to electrical resistance. The resistor in the circuit on the left corresponds to the pipe on the preceding two pages. There is an electric potential difference of 12 V across the resistor. The current in the resistor is 1.2 A.

Now set the emf of the battery to 24 V and observe the changes in the circuit.

When you have done this correctly, the potential difference across the resistor will have doubled, to 24 V, and so will have the current in the resistor, to 2.4 A. Thus, as in the case of water flowing through a pipe, the current in the resistor is proportional to the potential difference across the resistor.

As in the fluid flow case, one defines the resistance R of the resistor by

I = (1/R) DV.

Solving for R, this equation becomes

R = (DV)/I.

The SI-unit of electrical resistance is the ohm, symbol: W. A resistor that requires a potential difference of 100 V to produce a current of 2 A has a resistance of 100/2 = 50 W.
