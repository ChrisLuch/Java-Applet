DC Circuits

This session is titled "DC Circuits". 'DC' stands for 'directed current'. This means that the current in a circuit is always in one direction. If a battery is the source of potential difference in a circuit, the currents in the circuit will be DC.

In most households around the world, people are provided with alternate currents (AC). These are currents that alternate in direction. In North America, the frequency the current is 60 Hz. This means that the current changes direction every 1/100th of a second. Elsewhere, the frequency is 60 Hz.

As long as one is dealing only with resistors and light bulbs and does not include capacitors and inductors in a circuit, the principles of DC circuits can be applied without change to AC circuits, as long as one ignores the fact that the currents are constantly changing direction and the potential differences are constantly changing sign. It turns out that one can work with averaged current and potential difference values, so-called rms values, as if they were DC quantities.

Now, in your Notebook, summarize the essential features of DC circuits, the concepts needed to deal with these circuits, and their explanation in terms of the fluid flow analogy.
