DC Circuits

Potential. When the pump lifts an amount of fluid of mass m from the bottom to the top through a vertical distance h, it increases the fluid's gravitational potential energy in lifting the fluid, by the amount mgh.

The increase in the potential energy per mass lifted, which is equal to

mgh/m = gh,

is called the change in gravitational potential as one goes from the bottom to the top of the pump.

Distinguish between gravitational potential and gravitational potential energy. If the vertical lift of the pump is h = 5.0 m, the gravitational potential at the top of the pump is gh = 9.8x5.0 = 49 m2 s-2 greater than at the bottom. Up to a constant factor, g, the change in gravitational potential between the bottom and top of the pump is just the elevation change.

We have defined the change in gravitational potential. What about the gravitational potential itself? Just like gravitational potential energy, one can choose it to be zero at a convenient point. Let that point be at the bottom of the pump. The gravitational potential at the top of the pump is then equal to gh = 49 m2 s-2.

The important thing about the gravitational potential is that it is unrelated to the mass m. It simply characterizes points in the circuit, no matter whether there is a mass there or not. It is a property of the gravitational field created by the earth. It has a definite value at every point in the circuit (and outside the circuit). This is equal to gy where y is the elevation of that point relative to the point at the bottom of the pump.

If one multiplies the potential at a point by the mass m of fluid at that point, one gets the potential energy of the fluid mass at that point.

The concept of electric potential is analogous. If a positive charge q moves through a battery from one battery terminal to the other, the battery does work on the charge and increases the electric potential energy of the charge. The change in electric potential energy of the charge per charge is defined as the change in electric potential as one goes from one battery terminal to the other. Using the fluid flow analogy, electric potential change can be visualized as elevation change.

Again, the electric potential (like elevation) is a quantity that is defined at every point in an electric circuit (and at points outside the circuit) and that is independent of any charge present at that point. The electric potential is a property of the electric field that exists in the circuit and elsewhere.

The SI-unit of the electric potential is the volt (V). A 9-V battery is a battery that has a 9-V potential difference between its terminals. Read about emf on Page 7.
