Electric Circuits

Potential. When the pump lifts an amount of fluid of mass m from the bottom to the top through a vertical distance h, it increases the fluid's gravitational potential energy by the amount mgh. We will need another quantity, the gravitational potential, which is related to the gravitational potential energy and defined as follows.

The increase in the potential energy per unit mass lifted, which is equal to

mgh/m = gh,

is called the change in gravitational potential as one goes from the bottom to the top of the pump.

Distinguish between gravitational potential and gravitational potential energy! If the vertical lift of the pump is h = 5.0 m, the gravitational potential at the top of the pump is by gh = 9.8x5.0 = 49 m2 s-2 (or 49 J/kg) greater than at the bottom. The change in gravitational potential between the bottom and top of the pump is nothing but the elevation change, except for the constant factor g.

We have defined the change in gravitational potential. What about the gravitational potential itself? Just like gravitational potential energy, one can choose it to be zero at a convenient point. Let that point be at the bottom of the pump. The gravitational potential at the top of the pump is then equal to gh = 49 m2 s-2.

Here is an important feature of the gravitational potential: it does not depend on the mass m. The potential characterizes points in the fluid circuit, no matter whether there is a mass there or not. It is a property of the gravitational field created by the earth. It has a definite value at every point in the circuit (and outside the circuit). If one takes the potential to be zero at the bottom of the pump, the gravitational potential at a point of elevation y relative to the bottom of the pump is equal to gy.

If one multiplies the potential at a point by the mass m of of a fluid particle at that point, one gets the potential energy of the fluid particle at that point.

The concept of electric potential is analogous. If a positive charge q moves through a battery from one battery terminal to the other, the battery does work on the charge and increases the electric potential energy of the charge. The change in electric potential energy per unit charge, as charge moves from one battery terminal to the other, is defined as the change in electric potential as one goes from one terminal to the other. Using the fluid flow analogy, electric potential change can be visualized as elevation change.

Again, the electric potential (like elevation) is a quantity that is defined at every point in an electric circuit (and at points outside the circuit) and that is independent of any charge present at that point. The electric potential is a property of the electric field present in the circuit and elsewhere.

The SI-unit of the electric potential is the volt (V). A 9-V battery is a battery that has a 9-V potential difference between its terminals. Read about emf on Page 7.
