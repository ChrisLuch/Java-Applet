Electric Circuits

Energy Conservation. In raising the fluid from the bottom to the top, the pump gives it gravitational potential energy. This energy is dissipated in the form of heat when the fluid encounters friction while moving through the sloping narrow pipe. Note that potential energy is not converted into kinetic energy as the fluid moves down the pipe because the constancy of the current along the pipe implies constant fluid speed, assuming the pipe's diameter is constant.

Actually, we will assume that fluid speeds are so small throughout the circuit that the kinetic energy of the fluid is negligible altogether. A narrow pipe can bring this about. For electric circuits, it is an excellent assumption to neglect the kinetic energy of the moving charged particles.

Related fact. What do you think is the speed with which electrically charged particles move in a typical electric circuit, assuming they all have the same speed and all move in the same direction? This may surprise you, but the speed is incredibly small. Its order of magnitude is 1 meter per hour! The speed is not something approaching the speed of light. What is fast is the speed with which changes in one part of a circuit are transmitted to another part. E.g., if a switch is closed in a circuit, the electric charges in a light bulb in the circuit start moving and the bulb starts burning almost immediately, not hours after the switch was closed.
