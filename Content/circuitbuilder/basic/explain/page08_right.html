Electric Circuits

Resistance. What determines the magnitude of the current in the fluid flow circuit shown on the left? Let's assume the pump moves just enough fluid to the higher elevation to supply what is drained away from that point by the sloping narrow pipe. In other words, the pump is not putting pressure on the fluid at the entrance to the sloping pipe. This situation corresponds to the electric case.

The agent that moves the fluid through the narrow pipe is gravity, and the rate at which gravity is able to move fluid through the narrow pipe determines the magnitude of the current in the entire circuit. One says the pipe has resistance. (Let us assume that the horizontal pipe is quite wide and resistance-less and therefore does not need to be sloped.)

The resistance of the narrow pipe is defined so that it is inversely proportional to the current in the pipe. You will agree that the current will be smaller and therefore the resistance greater if the pipe is made narrower or longer. One can show that the resistance is proportional to the length of the pipe.

The resistance is due to friction and causes the potential energy of the fluid to be dissipated in the pipe in the form of heat as the fluid moves through the pipe.

Similar to the fluid circuit shown, in the electric circuit illustrated here the wires and battery have negligible resistance, but the heating coil is made of different material and has quite a bit of resistance. Therefore, the coil determines the magnitude of the current in the circuit (given the battery emf), and all of the electric potential energy gained by charge when moving through the battery is given up by the charge and converted into heat as the charge moves through the resistive coil.
