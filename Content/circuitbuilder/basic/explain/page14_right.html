Electric Circuits

Physicists (and electrical engineers) use schematic circuit diagrams, like the one at bottom left, to represent the circuit represented by the more realistic looking drawing at top left.

The symbol with the long and short bar represents a battery. The long bar represents the terminal with higher potential (the so-called 'positive' pole) and the short bar the terminal with the lower potential (the 'negative' pole). (The battery symbol is misleading because it looks as if the circuit is interrupted inside the battery. This is not the case in reality. Charges can move inside the battery from one terminal to the other.) The straight lines are wires, and you will be able to recognize the light bulb.

The schematic diagram does not indicate how wires must be connected to a real light bulb, with its thread on the side and metal contact at the base. So, it can be challinging in the lab to connect the elements of a circuit as indicated in a circuit diagram.

Nevertheless, circuit diagrams are extremely useful. For example, the diagram here shows that a light bulb has two contact points, not one. It does not indicate that the circuit is continued inside the bulb from one contact point to the other. Neither does the battery symbol indicate that the circuit is continued inside the battery, via the conducting battery fluid. Thus, it takes a bit of practice to interpret schematic circuit diagrams correctly. They are not the whole story.
