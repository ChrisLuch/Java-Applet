Electric Circuits

DC vs. AC Circuits

The electric circuits on the preceding pages have all been DC Circuits. 'DC' stands for 'directed current'. This means that the current in such a circuit is always in one direction. If a battery is the source of potential difference in a circuit, the currents in the circuit will be DC.

In most households around the world, people are provided with alternating currents (AC currents). These are currents that alternate in direction. In North America, the frequency of household currents is 60 Hz. This means that the current changes direction every 1/120th of a second and goes through a complete cycle 60 times every second. In many other parts of the world, the frequency is 50 Hz.

As long as a circuit includes only resistors and light bulbs and no capacitors and inductors, the principles of DC circuits can be applied without change to AC circuits, provided one ignores the fact that the currents and potential differences are constantly changing sign. It turns out that one can deal with such AC circuits simply by using averaged current and potential difference values, so-called rms values, as if they were DC quantities. The 120-V AC potential difference supplied to North American households is such an rms potential difference.
