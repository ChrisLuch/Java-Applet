Electric Circuits

Emf. The emf of a battery is defined as the work done on a charge q when the charge moves through the battery, divided by q. (Emf stands for the quaint and misleading term 'electro-motive force.)

If there is no 'friction' (internal resistance) inside the battery, causing some of the work done by the battery to be dissipated as heat inside the battery, the battery emf is equal to the potential difference between the battery terminals. If the battery does have internal resistance, the terminal potential difference is less than the emf. Reason: If some of the work done by the battery on a charge q is dissipated as heat inside the battery, less than the total work done remains to increase the charge's electrical potential energy, by energy conservation.

One often sees the symbol e for emf. e is the Greek letter 'epsilon'. In MAP, emf is also denoted by V0. The SI-unit for emf is the same as for electric potential, namely, the volt (V).

The picture illustrates a situation that can easily arise when a car battery whose emf is 12 V is used to start up the engine. In this situation, when there is a current in the circuit, the battery terminal potential difference is somewhat less than the battery emf of 12 V. The emptier the battery gets, the smaller the terminal potential difference will be and the less able the battery will be to turn over the starter motor. This is so because the internal resistance of the battery increases as the battery gets emptier. The emf always remains equal to 12 V.
