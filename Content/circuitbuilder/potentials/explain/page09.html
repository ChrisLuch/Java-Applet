Electric Potentials and Potential Differences

The potential at point A is 0 V, and it is 12 V at point B.  Thus, the potential change in going from A to B is:

VB - VA = 12 - 0 = 12 V.

This time the potential change in the direction of the current is positive. In terms of the analogy between electric potential and elevation, why should this be so?

On a hike in the mountains, if you leave your camp in the morning and return in the evening, you will have gone uphill some of the time, and downhill at other times. The sum of all the elevation changes will be zero, because at the end of the day your elevation is the same as at the beginning of the day.

Electric potential is analogous to elevation. Thus, in going around an electric circuit, when one returns to the starting point one returns to the same potential, and the sum of the potential changes around the circuit must be zero.

In the present circuit, we have two stretches of wire where the potential changes are zero, we have to go across a resistor where the potential change is -12 V, and we have to go across a battery where the potential change is +12 V. This adds up to a total potential change around the circuit equal to 0 V.

This can be generalized to one of the two fundamental laws of DC circuits:

Kirchhoff's Loop Rule

The sum of the potential changes around any closed loop in a DC circuit is equal to zero.
