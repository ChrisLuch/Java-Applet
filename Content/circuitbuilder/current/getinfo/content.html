Electric Current

Definition

Electric current is a quantity that is defined at every point in a circuit and can have different values at different points in a circuit (in different branches of a circuit). Imagine a cross section through a circuit element (wire, resistor, battery, etc.) at a given point of a circuit. The current I at that point is defined as the rate in time at which electric charge is crossing the cross section at that point, i.e., as the ratio

I = dQ/dt

where dQ is the amount of charge passing through the cross section in the interval dt.

The SI-unit of current is the ampere (short form: amp), which has the symbol 'A'.

If an amount of charge dQ = 2.4x10-4 C passes a given point in a time interval dt = 1.2x10-3 s, the current at that point is equal to

I = (2.4x10-4)/(1.2x10-3) = 0.2 A .

Kirchhoff's Junction Rule (Current Law)

At a junction (node) in a circuit, the sum of the currents going into the junction is equal to the sum of the currents leaving the junction.

This law is a consequence of electric charge conservation. The amount of charge going into a junction is equal to the amount coming out, in a given time interval.
