Solution

Set It Up and Solve It. Let the common resistance of bulbs A and B be R. In the top circuit, bulb A has another bulb of the same resistance R in series with it. Thus the potential difference across each bulb is V0/2.

The resistance of the combination of two in-parallel bulbs in the bottom circuit is less than R. In fact, it is equal to R/2. (See Resistance under Get Information. You will find an equation that lets you calculate this resistance. You can also review the theory of in-parallel resistors in Electric Circuits/Resistance/Explain It.)

The potential differences across resistors in series are proportional to the respective resistances. Thus, in the bottom circuit, the potential difference across bulb B is twice that across the in-parallel combination of bulbs, or (2/3)V0 across bulb B vs. (1/3)V0 across the in-parallel combination. (2/3)V0 is more than the (1/2)V0 across bulb A. Thus, bulb B burns brighter.
