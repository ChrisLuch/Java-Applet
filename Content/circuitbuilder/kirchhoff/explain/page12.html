Several Batteries

The circuit on the left contains three batteries and three resistors. Because of the way the batteries and resistors are positioned, the resistors are neither in-series nor in-parallel and we need to go back to Kirchhoff's Laws to calculate the currents in the circuit.

Question. How many different currents are there in this circuit and how many equations are necessary to calculate them?

Answer. There are three branches in the circuit and one current in each branch, thus a total of three currents. Each branch contains one battery. We need three equations to solve for these currents.

Question. How many current and voltage equations are necessary?

Answer. There will be one current equation, say, the equation at junction A. (The equation at junction B gives no new information.) In addition, there will be two voltage equations, one for the loop on the left, one for the loop on the right.

Try to write down these three equations, taking the positive directions in the three branches to be upward through all three batteries, and going around the two loops in the clockwise sense. When done, go to Page 13 to compare.
