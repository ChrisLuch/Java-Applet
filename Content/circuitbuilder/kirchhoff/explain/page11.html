The Wheatstone Bridge

Answer. Set R4 to 4 W in the simulation and you will find the cross current to be zero. Check it with an ammeter. Is there an easy way to obtain this value?

Yes. Let's start with the general expression for I5,

I5 = V0 (R2R3 - R1R4)/D .

This gives the condition for zero current I5 as

R2R3 = R1R4
or
R1/R3 = R2/R4 .

Substituting the given values for R1 to R3 gives R4 = 4 W.

One can understand the above ratio-condition for zero cross current without using the explicit expression above for I5. Consider a modified circuit that does not have the cross bridge. That circuit consists of two parallel branches, each with two resistors in series. These branches are voltage dividers, each dividing the 12-V potential difference that exists between points A and C. The potentials at the junction points B and D will be in between the potentials at A and C.

The ratio-condition R1/R3 = R2/R4 for zero cross current is just the condition under which points B and D divide the 12 V in the same manner in the two branches, so that the potentials at B and D are equal. If the potentials are equal at B and D, then reconnecting the cross bridge between these points will not cause a current in the bridge.

Finally some comments about the Wheatstone Bridge. The Wheatstone bridge lets you measure a resistance by comparing it against other resistances.

In a Wheatstone Bridge, the series combination of resistors R2 and R4 consists of a single resistance wire with a sliding contact at point D. By sliding this contact point, one changes the ratio R2/R4. The ratio can be measured by simply measuring the lenghts of resistance wire on either side of point D. Suppose resistance R3 is unknown and R1 is known. One adjusts the contact point D so that the cross current is zero, measures the ratio R2/R4, and then calculates R3 from the ratio-condition.
