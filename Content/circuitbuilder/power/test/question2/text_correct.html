Yes, 2.7 W of power is dissipated in the 6-W resistor.

Plan It. To calculate the power dissipated in a resistor one needs to know two of three quantities: the resistance of the resistor, the current in the resistor, the potential difference across the resistor. We are given the resistance and therefore need one of the other two in addition. We will calculate the current, which is the same everywhere in this circuit of in-series resistors, from the total resistance in the circuit and the battery voltage V0.

Set It Up and Solve It. The two resistors in series have a combined resistance Rtot equal to

Rtot = 12 + 6 = 18 W .

By the definition of resistance, the current I in the circuit is related to the total resistance and the potential difference V0 across the total resistance by

I = V0/Rtot = 12/18 = 2/3 A.

The power P dissipated in the 6-W resistor is then equal to

P = I 2 R = (2/3)2x6 = 8/3 = 2.7 W.

Think About It. Let's check this result by using an alternate solution that treats the two in-series resistors as a potential divider. (See Electric Circuits/Potential/Test Yourself/Potential Divider 1 for more detail on potential dividers.)

The 12-V total potential difference is the sum of the potential differences V6 and V12 across the in-series 6-W and 12-W resistors, respectively,

V6 + V12 = 12 V.

These two potential differences are in proportion to the two resistances,

V6/V12 = 6/12 = 1/2.

It is easy to solve these two equations for the two unknowns V6 and V12 and to verify the solutions

V6 = 4 V V12 = 8 V .

Then the power dissipated in the 6-W resistor is

P = V62/R6 = 42/6 = 8/3 = 2.7 W.

This is the same result as before.
