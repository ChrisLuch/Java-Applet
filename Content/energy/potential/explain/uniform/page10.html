Potential Energy Quantitative

On Page 9, when calculating the kinetic energy at y = 10 m, we started from the equation

KE10 - KE6 = - (PE10 - PE6).

Using this equation, we would have obtained the same result KE10 = 14 J, if instead of PE = mgy we had used for the potential energy an expression with an added constant, say,

PE = mgy + 15 J .

The additional constant of 15 J would have canceled when taking the difference PE10 - PE6.

Let's go back to the basic definition of potential energy on Page 7. There the change in potential energy was defined via

DPE = - Wgrav.

This equation defines the potential energy only to within an additive constant, because a change in the potential energy is not affected if all values of the potential energy are increased by the same amount.

By adding a constant to the potential energy, one changes the elevation at which the potential energy is equal to zero. To be able to do so is often convenient. An experimenter working on the fifth floor of a building may want to take the potential energy to be zero on that floor, while another experimenter working on the first floor may want to take the potential energy to be zero on that floor of the building. As long as one sticks to one convention consistently, throughout a calculation, it does not matter where one chooses the potential energy to be zero.

In the simulation, you can adjust the zero-point of the potential energy by dragging the energy scale up or down. Do the calculation from Page 9 with a different setting of the energy scale and convince yourself that this will not affect the value of the kinetic energy at y = 10 m.

Similarly, it is arbitrary where one chooses the origin of the y axis. That axis too can be dragged upward or downward.

The general expression for the gravitational potential energy of an object of mass m near the surface then is

PE = mgy + const

where the value of the added constant depends on where one has chosen the potential energy to be zero and where one has chosen the origin of the y axis to be.
