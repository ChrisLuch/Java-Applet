Potential Energy due to Gravitational Interaction

Object near the earth's surface. Let the two objects interacting gravitationally be the earth and a second object of mass m near the earth's surface. Let the direction pointing vertically upward be the y-direction, and let the origin y = 0 of the y-axis be somewhere near the earth's surface. It does not matter where exactly we put y = 0. (See below about additive constant in the potential energy.)

The gravitational potential energy PE of the earth/object pair if the object is near the earth's surface is then equal to

PE = mgy + const (1)

where y is the y-coordinate of the object's position and g is the magnitude of the acceleration due to gravity at the earth's surface.

Comments

  1. The added constant in Expression (1) can be chosen to have any value. Often it is convenient to set it equal to zero. Choosing the origin of the y-axis to be higher or lower is equivalent to choosing a different value for this constant.

  2. Expression (1) depends on both objects, the earth and the second object:

    • the value of g depends on the mass M of the earth and the distance r between the object of mass m and the center of the earth
    • the factor m obviously relates to the second object
    • y is a measure of the separation between the earth and the second object

    This illustrates that the potential energy (1) belongs to both the earth and the second object.

  3. It is customary to refer to Expression (1) as the "potential energy of the object of mass m" and not as the "potential energy of the earth/object pair". This custom is only a convenience and does not express the correct understanding of Expression (1).

  4. Expression (1) is an approximate expression for the potential energy. It is true only as long as one can ignore the variation of g with elevation. (g decreases as one goes higher, but very little over a distance of a few hundred meters.) If the object is moved to substantial distances from the surface of the earth, say, a few hundred kilometers or more, Expression (1) must be replaced by Expression (2) below.

  5. Expression (1) implies that the potential energy does not change when one moves the object of mass m horizontally. PE depends only on the vertical displacement of the object from the earth's surface.

  6. The SI-unit of potential energy is the same as that for any energy, namely, the joule (J).

Object at an arbitrary distance from the earth's surface. In this case, the potential energy of the object/earth pair is given by

PE(r) = - GMm/r, r > R (2)

where r is the object's distance from the center of the earth, G is the universal gravitational constant, M is the mass of the earth, and m the mass of the object.

Comments

  1. Expression (2) for the potential energy and the corresponding Graph 2 look very different from Expression (1) and its Graph 1. How come? Are they not both supposed to give the potential energy of the earth/object pair?

    Yes, they are. Let's compare them in detail. First of all, they give different values for PE at r = R. If const = 0 in Expression (1), then this expression gives the value 0 at r = R while Expression (2) gives the value -GMm/R. A constant can always be added to the potential energy without affecting anything that is observable experimentally. Only changes in the potential energy are observable, and adding a constant term to a potential energy does not affect by how much the potential energy changes as one goes from one point to another. Thus, let us add the constant GMm/R to Expression (2). This modifies Expression (2) to

    PE(r) = - GMm/r + GMm/R, r > R (3)

    Expression (3) for PE is represented by the blue curve in Graph 3 below. The blue curve in Graph 3 is the same as the blue curve in Graph 2, but shifted upward by the constant amount GMm/R.

    Graph 3

    Now let us compare Expression (3) and Graph 3 to Expression (1) and Graph 1. Both give the same value 0 for r = R, but Graph 3 is curved while Graph 1 is a straight line. The straight line in Graph 1 is identical with the purple straight line in Graph 3. This line is the tangent to the blue curve at r = R. This straight line approximates the curve in the vicinity of r = R, i.e., near the earth's surface. The blue curve represents the potential energy exactly, for all r > R, while the purple straight line is the linear approximation to this curve valid only near r = R. Similarly, Expressions (2) or (3) are exact expressions for the potential energy, valid for all r > R, while Expression (1) is an approximation that is valid only near r = R.

  2. Expressions (2) and (3) are exact if one assumes the earth to be a perfect sphere with a uniform mass distribution. Under these assumptions, for r > R, the earth acts gravitationally as if all of its mass were concentrated at its center. In reality, the earth has a somewhat larger radius at the equator than at the poles and its mass distribution is not entirely uniform either. Thus, the true potential energy contains small correction terms to Expressions (2) and (3).

  3. For r < R, i.e., if the object is inside the earth, Expressions (2) and (3) no longer apply, because it is not true for r < R that the earth acts as if all of its mass were located at the earth's center.

Interaction between two point particles. Suppose two point particles of masses m1 and m2 a distance r apart are interacting gravitationally. The potential energy due to this interaction is given by

PE(r) = - Gm1m2/r, r > 0 (4)

This equation is valid for all r > 0. The constant G is the universal gravitational constant equal to

G = 6.67x10-11 N m2 kg-2 .(5)
