Potential Energy due to Electrostatic Interaction

Charged particle in uniform electric field. Suppose a point particle of charge q is placed in a uniform static electric field of magnitude E pointing in the negative y-direction. This field is due to a distribution of fixed source charges, and the force experienced by the given point particle in the electric field is the result of the interaction of the particle with all of these source charges. The field concept summarizes the effect of the individual interactions between the particle and the source charges.

If the particle's charge q is positive, the given situation is analogous to that on Page 2 where a particle of mass m is in the uniform gravitational field of magnitude g near the surface of the earth. In both cases, the force points in the direction of the field, which is in the negative y-direction in both cases.

Therefore, the expression for the electric potential energy of the charged particle is analogous to Expression (1) on Page 2 for the gravitational potential energy. The gravitational force acting on the object on Page 2 has magnitude mg and the electric force on the charged particle has magnitude qE. Thus, replacing mg by qE in Expression (1) from Page 2 gives us the electric potential energy of the charged particle as

PE = qEy + const (1)

where y is the y-coordinate of the particle's position.

This expression can be generalized to one that applies to two situations, those in which the electric field points either in the negative or positive y-direction. We only need to replace the magnitude E of the field by the negative of the y-component Ey of the field. Thus,

PE = -qEyy + const(2)

is the more general expression for the potential energy of the particle.

One needs the negative of the y-component because Ey = -E when the field is pointing in the negative y-direction, as it is in Equation (1). The minus sign in Equation (2) ensures that both Equations (1) and (2) give the same value for PE when the field is in the negative y-direction.

When the electric field points in the positive y-direction, Expression (1) is no longer valid, but Expression (2) still is. The reason is that Ey changes sign when the field reverses direction so that the potential energy as given by Equation (2) changes sign as well, except for the unimportant added constant. The potential energy must change sign when the field and therefore the force on the given particle reverse direction.

One difference between the electric and gravitational cases is that electric charge can be either positive or negative while mass is never negative. When the charge of the particle changes sign, the electric force acting on the particle reverses so that the potential energy should again change sign. Note that Expression (2) does that, except for the unimportant added constant. Thus, Expression (2) gives the potential energy of a point particle in an electric field that points in the positive or negative y-direction, no matter what the sign of the charge is.

Of course, if the electric field is either in the x or z directions, y and Ey in Expression (2) must be replaced by x and Ex or z and Ez, respectively.

Interaction between two charged point particles. Suppose we have two point particles carrying charges q1 and q2 that are a distance r apart. The particles are interacting electrostatically, i.e., via the Coulomb interaction. The potential energy due to this interaction is given by

PE(r) = kq1q2/r, r > 0 (3)

where k is the constant in Coulomb's force law,

k = 8.99x109 N m2 C-2 .(4)

Comments

  1. Equation (3) is analogous to Equation (4) on Page 2 for the potential energy of two gravitationally interacting point particles.

  2. Expression (3) above and Expression (4) on Page 2 differ by an overall sign. The reason for this difference is that when both particles have positive charge they repel each other electrostatically while two particles with (necessarily) positive masses attract each other gravitationally.

    When the two charges have opposite sign, the particles attract each other electrostatically and Expression (3) above gives a negative value for the electric potential energy, just like Expression (4) on Page 2 which always gives a negative value for the gravitational potential energy.
