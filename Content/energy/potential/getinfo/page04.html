General Definition of Potential Energy

  1. Like all forms of energy, potential energy is a scalar quantity.

  2. Like all forms of energy, potential energy has as its SI-unit the joule (J).

  3. Potential energy is a quantity that is associated with the interaction between objects. A single object by itself does not have a potential energy. (Also please note that only certain kinds of interaction have potential energies associated with them. Others cannot be characterized this way. See Item 6 below.)

    One often encounters situations in which a single object is located in either a fixed gravitational or electrostatic field. One can define the potential energy of the particle in such a field. What does this mean in view of the statement that potential energy is associated with the interaction between objects? A field is always due to 'sources', i.e., other particles, and the field concept is simply a convenient way of summarizing the interaction between the given particle and the source particles. Thus, the 'potential energy of the particle in the field' is really the potential energy of interaction between the particle and the source particles. At least this is the way one conceptualizes things in particle mechanics. (In field theory one looks at things differently.)

  4. Let us define the potential energy of a particle in a fixed gravitational or electrostatic field. The field will exert a force on the particle. Suppose the particle moves from point A to point B. Then this force will do an amount of work WA to B on the particle. One defines the change DPE in the particle's potential energy, as the particle moves from A to B, by

    DPE = PEB - PEA = - WA to B .(1)

  5. Equation (1) defines only the change in the potential energy, not the potential energy at a given point. Equation (1) gives for the potential energy at point B the expression

    PEB = - WA to B + PEA .(2)

    One can set the potential energy when the particle is at a given point A equal to some arbitrary value. Then the potential energy at any other point B is defined uniquely by Expression (2).

  6. The forces exerted by a gravitational or electrostatic field on a particle have the special property that the work done by such a force on the particle does not depend on the path taken by the particle, as the particle moves from any point A to any point B. Such forces are called conservative forces.

    One can define a potential energy only for conservative forces, because if WA to B depends on the path connnecting A to B then this work and PEB, as given by Expression (2), do not have unique values. The potential energy is, however, supposed to have a unique value at a given point, except for the arbitrary additive constant.

    An example of a force that is not conservative is friction.

  7. If a particle is interacting with a second much larger object that is so large that it can be assumed to remain at rest while the particles are interacting, then the potential energy of this two-body system can be defined as above. The large object creates a force field in which the small object moves. (Example: ball near the surface of the earth.) The work done on the small object by this force field is the negative change of the potential energy of the two-body system. (This is assuming the force is conservative. Otherwise, one does not define a potential energy.)

  8. If two particles are interacting whose masses are comparable, so that both particles' motions will be affected by the interaction between the particles, then the potential energy change of this two-particle system is still defined by Equation (1). However, now A denotes not only the initial position of one of the particles, but the initial positions of both particles. Similarly, B now denotes the final positions of both particles, and WA to B the work done on both particles as they move from A to B. This work is the sum of the work done on Particle 1, by the force exerted on this particle by Particle 2, plus the work done on Particle 2, by the force exerted on this particle by Particle 1.

  9. Because of the minus sign in Equation (1), the potential energy increases in the direction that is opposite to the force of interaction. E.g., if a charged particle in an electric field has an electric force acting on it that is in the negative y-direction, then the electric potential energy increases in the positive y-direction.

    Vice versa, the force exerted on a particle in a field points in the direction from high to low potential energy; more exactly, in the direction in which the potential energy decreases most rapidly. (This has significance in two or three dimensions. In one dimension, there can be only one direction in which the potential energy can decrease.)

The expressions for the potential energy on Pages 2 and 3 can all be derived from Equation (1) if one substitutes the appropriate forces. When variable forces are involved, calculating the work WA to B requires working out an integral.
